There are a lot of machine learning courses that teach you to call a function and read the output. This is not one of them. At the MSc level, I expect students to understand what is happening inside the model — the geometry of a decision boundary, the assumptions baked into a loss function, the reason a random forest behaves differently from a single tree. That understanding is what allows you to adapt when the standard approach does not work, which in practice is often.

We cover six topics — regression, logistic regression, KNN and clustering, random forests, dimensionality reduction, and reinforcement learning — and we go deep on each one rather than skimming across many. Every topic is connected to finance: predicting returns, segmenting portfolios, compressing high-dimensional market data, building agents that learn from price signals. The goal is not to produce a list of algorithms you have heard of, but a small set you genuinely understand and can use well.

The group assignment is worth 60% of the grade for a reason. Taking a self-sourced dataset, choosing the right methods, applying them correctly, and writing up what you found — that is closer to what actual research and industry work look like than any exam. Each lecture also comes in two versions: a full technical treatment for those who want the mathematical detail, and a more accessible version that builds intuition first. Both matter, and both are available to every student.
