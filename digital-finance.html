Finance is being rebuilt from the ground up — and most of that reconstruction is happening in code. This course takes that seriously. We do not treat technology as an add-on to traditional finance education. We start from the problems the industry is actually trying to solve: reducing friction in payments, building trust without intermediaries, automating compliance, managing risk when markets move faster than human judgment can follow.

My background is in quantitative finance — mathematics at ETH, then years working at Goldman Sachs, Credit Suisse, and other institutions where these questions were not academic. That experience shapes how this course is taught. The theory matters, but only insofar as it helps you understand what is actually happening in markets and institutions today. We cover AI and ML not as buzzwords but as tools with specific capabilities and specific limitations — and we are honest about both.

The course runs in three editions to serve different stages of a career: a classic structured version for BSc students building their foundations, a problem-based version organised around the real challenges practitioners face, and a PhD seminar for researchers working at the frontier. Across all three, the goal is the same — to give you a way of thinking about digital finance that holds up when the technology changes, which it will.
